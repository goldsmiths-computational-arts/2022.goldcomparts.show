Eden Chahal

A Tale of 10.000 Cities

Before completing an MFA in Computational Arts at the University of Goldsmiths, Eden was trained in architecture in Paris and Madrid. Instead of building the physical environment, her practice now focuses on constructing narrative tools, with cities as a subject and space as a material. She investigates human experiences of inhabiting places, to answer the question: what remains of architecture when it becomes a memory? With writing at its core, her work matches diverse techniques and media to imagine new tools by combining fixed and moving images, spatial and sound installations, physical computing, interactive fictions, XR.

A film written with code, English, music and architecture about human experiences of inhabiting cities.

A Tale of 10.000 cities is a film that contains many possible portraits of cities. It is London that is filmed, it is used as a material, an alphabet with which a language is built. Code is incorporated into the structure of the stories, guided by an Object Oriented Programming methodology. This work is about what happens when a language is hybridised with another. The spectator never sees the character, they discover them through their perception of the city, and a voice-over narration of one of a thousand tales. The purpose is to question how computation can be used to generate a grammar, with cities as an alphabet for a new language. Programming here allows us to get close an important aspect of cities. The fact that they are hard to grasp as a homogeneous entity, and that they are asynchronous, they hold at the same moment an infinite number of possibilities and experiences. Code allows to convey this fundamental idea. Instead of developing a way to grasp its complexity, it instead shows a reality: cities are not graspable in a glance, and it is part of what makes them fascinating. It is also about using technology within the production of mediums that are already widely democratised and part of our common imaginaries.
